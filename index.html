When does a world model actually control?

LeWorldModel uses a planning loop inspired by Model Predictive Control. This project applies classical control theory — stability, convergence, constraints — to rigorously characterise what that loop actually does, and where it breaks.

Background

What is this project?

LeWorldModel (LeWM) is a compact, 15-million-parameter robot learning system that plans actions by imagining future states in a learned latent space. Its authors describe its planning loop as inspired by Model Predictive Control — a classical control technique with decades of theory behind it.

More precisely, LeWM belongs to the family of Nonlinear MPC (NMPC): its plant model is a deep neural network, its cost landscape is non-convex, and its optimizer is a derivative-free sampling method (CEM) rather than a QP solver. This classification matters — it defines exactly which body of control theory is relevant, and which requirements LeWM does not yet satisfy.

This project asks: what does that planning loop actually look like when you put it under a control-theory microscope? We map every component of an MPC block diagram onto its LeWM equivalent, characterise the open-loop response before any closed-loop evaluation, and apply NMPC-appropriate stability and robustness tests. The goal is not to critique LeWM — it performs strongly on benchmarks. The goal is to understand it rigorously.

Key Finding

7 of 14 classical MPC components have no equivalent in LeWM. All three missing constraint mechanisms are structural gaps. LeWM is most precisely classified as an uncharacterised Nonlinear MPC — none of the standard NMPC stability requirements are satisfied or tested.

Approach

We instrument the CEM planner with logging hooks and apply control-theoretic metrics directly — no retraining, no new model, no specialised hardware. Reproducible on a single consumer GPU.

Research Questions

What we are investigating

Five research questions, ordered from empirical to theoretical. The first four drive the experimental work; the fifth points toward future contributions.

RQ1
Does latent distance meaningfully track physical progress toward the goal?
The planner uses latent distance as its only metric of progress. If this distance can decrease while the robot moves away from the goal, the planner's core assumption is violated. We measure the correlation between latent progress and actual task completion across episodes.
RQ2
How does prediction error accumulate across planning rollouts?
Every predicted latent state carries a small error. How does this error grow over a 10-step horizon? Does the planner visit ghost points — latent states with no real-world counterpart? At what horizon length does rollout drift become fatal?
RQ3
What are the convergence and stability properties of the MPC feedback loop?
Does the system converge smoothly to the goal, or does it oscillate, overshoot, or stall? How quickly does it recover from external perturbations? How does performance degrade as a function of goal distance and horizon length?
RQ4
Which of the 20 implicit assumptions are most frequently violated?
We enumerate 20 assumptions the LeWM planner makes implicitly. For each that is empirically testable, we design a targeted experiment. Which assumptions hold? Which break most often? Which violations correlate with planning failure?
RQ5
What constraints could be incorporated in latent space, and what would be gained?
An exploratory question. Can hard or soft constraints be co-encoded in the latent representation? What form would a latent hard constraint take? Can the data manifold itself serve as a natural feasibility constraint? This question bridges empirical analysis to future theoretical work on safe robot deployment.
Evaluation Framework

A three-horizon framework — and our scope

A complete evaluation of a latent-space NMPC planner spans three distinct horizons. This project claims and demonstrates Horizon 2 in full. Horizons 1 and 3 are documented as the complete framework.

Horizon 1
Design Evaluation
Was the controller built correctly according to NMPC theory? Pre-operational criteria that must hold for any closed-loop guarantee.
  • ✗Terminal cost as CLF
  • ✗Terminal constraint set
  • ✗Input-to-State Stability
  • ✗Horizon sufficiency proof
  • ✗Plant-model mismatch bound
Status: documented gap — all absent in LeWM
Horizon 2 — Our Scope
Operational Evaluation
Does the running controller behave correctly? Runtime tests applied to the deployed planner — no retraining required.
  • ✓Test 0: open-loop response
  • ✓Convergence & stability
  • ✓Perturbation recovery
  • ✓Manifold drift measurement
  • ✓ISS & horizon empirical tests
Status: demonstrated in full
Horizon 3
Deployment Evaluation
Does it hold up in the real world? Post-operational criteria requiring real robot hardware and out-of-distribution conditions.
  • →Sim-to-real gap
  • →Sensor noise & distribution shift
  • →Constraint satisfaction in real world
  • →Contact uncertainty
  • →Real-time replanning constraints
Status: scoped as future work
Horizon 2 — Operational Tests in Detail
Test 0 — Prerequisite
Open-Loop Response (Camacho Ch.2)
Step & Impulse Response in Latent Space
Apply constant action (step) or single large action (impulse) to the predictor. Compare predicted trajectory to real trajectory. The gap is a direct measure of plant model fidelity — must precede all closed-loop tests.
Testable — Day 1
MPC Object Creation
System Initialisation
Checkpoint loads, CEM configures, get_cost executes without error. Binary pass/fail — the baseline sanity check.
Testable — Day 3
Closed-Loop Internal Stability
Trajectory Contraction
For pairs of nearby starting states, do latent trajectories converge or diverge over time? Empirical analogue of eigenvalue analysis.
Testable — Day 3
Closed-Loop Nominal Stability
Latent Distance Convergence
Plot latent distance to goal vs MPC timestep. Measure convergence rate, oscillation frequency, and failure-to-converge rate across episodes.
Testable — Day 3
Steady-State Gains
Terminal Latent Offset
Measure latent distance at episode termination. Persistent non-zero distance = steady-state offset, the latent-space analogue of integral error.
Testable — Day 4
QP Hessian Validity
CEM Solution Consistency
Run CEM 10× from different seeds on the same state. High variance in selected actions signals a multimodal landscape where sampling is unreliable.
Testable — Day 4
Soft Constraints
Manifold Drift via SIGReg
Compute SIGReg Gaussian log-likelihood of each predicted latent. Track off-manifold drift fraction across rollout depth and episode outcome.
Testable — Day 4
NMPC: ISS Empirical
Bounded Response to Disturbances
Inject disturbances of increasing magnitude. Measure whether latent trajectory remains bounded. Empirical analogue of the Horizon 1 ISS criterion.
Testable — Day 5
NMPC: Horizon Sufficiency
Performance vs Horizon Length
Run evaluation at horizons 5, 10, 15, 20. Plot success rate and convergence speed. Identifies minimum reliable horizon — empirical analogue of Horizon 1 horizon sufficiency proof.
Gap — document only
Hard MV + State Constraints
No Constraints in LeWM
No rate constraints, no state constraints, no feasibility checking. Structural gaps documented — not implementation choices.
Implicit Assumptions

20 assumptions the planner makes without stating them

Structural analysis of the LeWM planning loop reveals 20 assumptions never formally stated in the paper. Each is a potential failure mode and a research question.

State Space (A1–A3)
  1. Z is a sufficient statistic for planning
  2. Data manifold is well-defined and stable
  3. Latent distance ≈ physical progress
Plant Model (A4–A7)
  1. Predictor is a good one-step approximation
  2. Errors do not grow catastrophically
  3. Dynamics are deterministic
  4. No external disturbances — process noise, unexpected contacts, and model mismatch are not modelled
Encoder (A8–A10)
  1. Observations are clean and consistent
  2. Each frame encodes independently
  3. No sim-to-real gap
Optimizer (A11–A13)
  1. CEM finds good solution in 30 iterations
  2. 300 samples adequately cover action space
  3. Cost landscape is smooth enough for CEM
Control Loop (A14–A17)
  1. Replanning every step is sufficient
  2. Horizon of 10 steps is adequate
  3. No constraints beyond action bounds
  4. Goal fully specified by one image
Stability (A18–A20)
  1. Closed loop converges to goal
  2. Encoder and planning errors are independent
  3. Warm starting improves performance
